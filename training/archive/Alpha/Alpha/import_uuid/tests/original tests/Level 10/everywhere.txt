In a quiet village nestled on the edge of a great forest, there was a little house with a single window that always glowed, even on the darkest nights. The house had been abandoned for as long as anyone could remember, but the light never dimmed. 

Children dared each other to peer inside, their faces pressed to the cold glass, only to see nothing but a bare wooden floor. Adults warned them away, whispering about strange disappearances and the peculiar stories told by those who had dared to step through the door.

The stories spoke of Storyland, a place where logic twisted and frayed, and time itself curled up like an old ribbon. There, the impossible was not only possible—it was ordinary. Roads stretched into the sky, rivers flowed backward, and characters plucked from the minds of dreamers wandered freely. Yet, no one could explain how the house’s light connected to the tales.

For Mara, the house was more than a mystery. It was a puzzle that tugged at the edges of her restless mind. She’d spent years wandering from town to town, never quite finding where she belonged. People called her a dreamer, a drifter, but she always felt something pulling her forward, just beyond her reach. When she arrived in the village, the glow of the window seemed to beckon her.

One stormy night, when the streets were empty, Mara stood before the little house. The rain soaked through her coat, but she didn’t feel it. Her eyes fixed on the light—a warm, golden glow, impossibly steady. She placed her hand on the doorknob, and it turned with surprising ease.

Inside, the light poured from the window like liquid sun. She stepped closer, squinting against its brilliance. The floor creaked beneath her feet, and the room seemed to stretch and shrink in uneven pulses. She felt an odd sensation in her chest, like the tug of an invisible thread.

As she reached the window, Mara realized the glow wasn’t coming from outside. It was pouring *through* the glass, like light in reverse. She placed her hand on the pane, and the world seemed to shift. The air grew thick with the scent of pine and ocean spray. The walls of the house faded into the mist.

When the mist cleared, she was no longer in the house. She stood on a narrow bridge made of woven starlight, stretching endlessly into the void. Below her, fragments of landscapes—mountains, cities, forests—floated like islands in a boundless sea. She felt no fear, only awe. 

In this place, movement wasn’t about walking or running. It was about *choosing.* The farther she let her mind wander, the closer she became to the places she imagined. With each step, she found herself somewhere new—standing atop a crystal tower, swimming through a sky of molten clouds, or walking a garden path where trees whispered secrets in languages she didn’t know.

But amid the wonder, she always felt the pull of something greater. A flicker of golden light, just at the edge of her vision. It danced away whenever she turned, teasing her to follow. She didn’t know if it was guiding her or leading her astray, but she chased it all the same.

One day, she found herself standing before another house. Its single window glowed with the same golden light. This time, she didn’t hesitate. She opened the door and stepped inside, only to find herself back where she had begun. The same creaking floor, the same empty room.

But now, she knew the truth.

The house wasn’t a gateway to Storyland. It *was* Storyland. The light wasn’t just a glow; it was the thread that connected her to everything else. In this place, there were no causes or effects, no beginnings or endings. She could be anywhere, if only she wandered far enough. And though she didn’t know what lay beyond the light, she realized that the journey itself was the story.

Mara smiled, turned, and stepped into the window’s glow once more.
